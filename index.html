About

He used to see his own life as a vulture. Now he sees it as a hummingbird.

Trey Toler talks about the stuff most people only admit in therapy — and he does it with the velocity of someone used to keeping a room engaged, and the sensitivity of someone who learned early how to read what isn't being said.

Raised in the American South by a single mother with chronic illness, he stepped into adult awareness young, and never fully stepped back out of it.

His debut memoir, Good Damage, is what came of that.

"Although Toler's absorbing volume doesn't shy away from the harsh realities of life, it is relentlessly optimistic."— Kirkus Reviews

BOOK

The Book

Good Damage

Tragedy. Lightly Polished, with a Side of Optimism

Devastating and funny. Dark and light. Good and damage. A memoir that refuses to separate them. Good Damage is the debut memoir from Trey Toler about the messy, unscripted "after" — after the illness, the loss, the burnout, and the exhausting performance of recovery.

At the heart of the story is his mother — her fierce beauty, her harrowing brain surgery, and her eventual absence. Instead of reframing the past with empty optimism, Trey writes from the messy space where many of us actually live, channeling the dark, earned humor of a stand-up comic who has learned that nothing pulls strangers into a room faster than the truth you didn't plan to admit.

Speaking

Trey Toler

Trey used to look at his own life like a vulture. Now he sees it like a hummingbird — and he's pretty sure you can learn the difference too.

Trey doesn't tell a recovery story. He tells the one that comes after it — the part nobody prepares you for, where the crisis is over and you still get to decide who you're going to be. One minute he's reading a chapter that breaks the room open, the next he's already found the joke hiding inside it. That range is the whole experience: a reading that lands like a gut-punch, a bit that has the same room howling thirty seconds later. Audiences leave laughing about things they didn't expect to laugh at, and thinking about the parts of their own story they've been avoiding.

"Trey writes the way he performs: honest, funny, and completely fearless."— Marshall Chiles, Comedian and Owner of Whiplash Comedy

The Experience

Bookstores & Reading Events

Every event is a reading and a set at once — a chapter that breaks the room open, then the joke that was hiding inside it, howling the room back thirty seconds later. One room, two reasons to be there. Before the night ends, Trey opens the floor: sixty seconds each, anyone who wants their own before-and-after heard. A few dozen strangers walk in. One audience walks out.

For Book Clubs

Trey joins the discussion in person. A Southern memoir built on church, his mother's death, and a stand-up career gives a book club two feelings to sit with at once — grief, and the humor that got him through it.

For Companies & Community Organizations

Trey built his career on the same instinct that built the book: you can train yourself to see differently. As former President of Fuse in Atlanta and a partnerships executive, he brings that instinct to keynotes on authenticity at work, adaptability, and the distance between performing well and being present. Teams leave with proof that what you choose to scan for changes what you find.

"People don't connect because you're flawless or fascinating. They connect because the honesty underneath it lets them recognize themselves."— Trey Toler, Good Damage

Tim Andrews of The Popcast on 95.5 WSB Atlanta says Trey "can make you laugh at something painful before you've even realized you're in it." That's the instinct behind every keynote and performance.
